Overview

Texas is the leading U.S. natural gas state. In 2024, Texas accounted for 28% of U.S. natural gas gross withdrawals, and EIA reported record Texas gross withdrawals of about 13 trillion cubic feet.

Natural gas is central to Texas energy because it fuels power generation, industrial activity, petrochemical production, exports, and pipeline infrastructure. In the atlas, natural gas is represented by producing fields, shale trends, gas hubs, storage areas, and processing-related facilities.

Major Regions
  • Barnett Shale

    North-Central Texas shale-gas area and one of the largest onshore natural gas fields in the United States.

  • Haynesville / Bossier Shale

    East Texas gas-bearing shale and tight-gas trend connected to the broader Haynesville system.

  • Permian Basin

    West Texas oil and gas basin where associated gas from oil development is a major part of the gas story.

Brief Historical Overview

Texas natural gas development began with conventional fields and expanded through major Panhandle and East Texas production. Gas infrastructure later grew around hubs, storage fields, pipelines, and processing facilities.

The modern shale-gas era transformed Texas gas development. The Barnett Shale became a major early shale-gas success, followed by growth in the Haynesville/Bossier, Eagle Ford, and associated gas from the Permian Basin.
